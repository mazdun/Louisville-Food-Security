Louisville Food Security

Measuring food insecurity accurately is challenging because it overlaps with a number of difficult-to-measure factors, like poverty, unemployment, median income, disability status and others. Also, those who are considered food insecure at one point in time might not be food insecure at other times. Often, individuals must make difficult (and ongoing) trade off decisions between paying housing or medical bills and buying nutritious food.

However, gaining a clear understanding of a community’s food ecosystem is essential to boosting its social and economic health.

Here are three broad categories to gain a deeper understanding of the food ecosystem in Louisville and its peer cities:

  • Food Insecurity: percentage of the population who did not have reliable access to food over the past year
  • Food Access: percentage of the population who had some barrier to accessing food over the past year — income constraints, limited vehicle access, etc.
  • Food Environment: overview of various places to buy food in Louisville and its peer cities

For a closer look at food insecurity in Louisville, check out this report from the Community Foundation of Louisville.

Food Insecurity

The food insecurity metric estimates how much of the population did not have reliable access to food during the past year. Those without reliable access to food tend to disproportionately suffer from diabetes and high blood pressure, among other negative health outcomes, according to Feeding America.

  • How Louisville’s Currently Doing: Nearly 13% of Louisville’s population experienced food insecurity last year. That’s better than most of Louisville’s peers.
  • How Louisville’s Performed Over Time: For much of the last decade, Louisville had largely been moving in tandem with its peers. Around 2018, it saw sharp decreases in its food insecurity rate. The city appears to have sustained those decreases during the pandemic, according to Feeding America projections.

On the other hand, the food insecurity rate among children is significantly higher. Unlike the overall food insecurity rate, the child food insecurity rate has actually worsened in recent years.

Child Food Insecurity

Food insecurity among children can be particularly devastating. It can lead to delayed development in young children and increase the risk of asthma, anemia, hyperactivity, anxiety and aggression in school-aged children, according to Feeding America.

  • How Louisville’s Currently Doing: Louisville currently ranks 9th in child food insecurity, with nearly 1 in 5 children experiencing food insecurity in the last year.
  • How Louisville’s Performed Over Time: Around 2018, the child food insecurity rate rose dramatically in Louisville. Before that, it had been holding steady. Indianapolis is the worst performer of the bunch, with its child food insecurity rate rising 0.2 percentage points since 2014. Louisville is not far behind, having seen only a modest 0.4 percentage point decrease in its rate over the same time period.

Food Access

Now, here’s a closer look at the food ecosystem in Louisville: the percentage of the population that is considered low-income and far from a grocery store, the percentage that does not have a vehicle and is far from a grocery store and the percentage that relies on certain food benefits and is far from a grocery store.

Limited Access to Grocery Stores

When looking at the food ecosystem, it’s important to consider not only those who live far from grocery stores but also those who live far from a grocery store and those who live on a low income.

  • How Louisville’s Currently Doing: Louisville currently ranks 7th on this metric, with about 6% of residents living with low incomes and far from grocery stores. That’s about half the rate of Louisville’s currently worst performing peer city, Birmingham.
  • How Louisville’s Performed Over Time: Louisville has seen its numbers getting worse over the past five years of most recent data. Generally, over the same time period, Louisville’s peers have been improving.

In terms of race, more than a quarter of White residents in Louisville have limited access to grocery stores, vs. 17.8% of Black residents and 15.6% of Hispanic residents. The numbers are significantly higher in Louisville’s peer cities. However, it’s important to note that this race data considers the entire population that is far from a grocery store (not just those living with low incomes). Therefore, the race data includes a percentage of the population that lives on a higher income and therefore can afford to drive greater distances to a grocery store.

Ranking

In rural communities, far from a grocery store means more than 10 miles; in non-rural areas, it’s more than 1 mile. The race data considers the entire population (not just those with low incomes) who live more than 10 miles from a grocery store.

Limited Access to Grocery Stores and No Vehicle

It becomes significantly more challenging to access nutritious food at a grocery store if a household lives far from a grocery store and does not have access to a vehicle. These households often must rely on public transportation or other transportation methods or shop at stores that are closer to them that also potentially have less nutritious food.

  • How Louisville’s Currently Doing: More than 2% of Louisville’s population lives more than a mile from a grocery store and does not have a vehicle. That puts it in the bottom half of its peer cohort.
  • How Louisville’s Performed Over Time: Over the last five years of most recent data, Louisville has more than doubled this rate. That makes it the worst performer of its peers.

  • How Louisville’s Performed Over Time: Louisville’s rate has increased (though only modestly) over the past five years in which data is available. However, its peer cities have seen their rates decrease. That makes Louisville the worst performer of its peers.

Food Environment

What does the food environment look like in Louisville? Here’s a breakdown of the number of grocery stores, convenience stores, fast food restaurants and farmers markets per 100,000 people.

Food Sources

The number of grocery stores and fast food restaurants have remained relatively constant since 2008. Even so, Louisville currently has more grocery stores per 100,000 people than its peer cities, on average. The number of convenience stores in Louisville and its peer cities has decreased.

Tackling Food Insecurity

Here’s an estimate from Feeding America and Nielsen of how much money (annually) is needed in Louisville and its peer cities to address food insecurity.

Budget shortfalls have decreased over time in Louisville and its peer cities. (It’s important to note that the percentage of people experiencing food insecurity has also decreased over this time.) In Louisville, the food insecure need about 46 million collectively to meet their food needs. That’s lower than the peer average of about 51 million.
